What Is a CMA (Comparative Market Analysis)?

A Comparative Market Analysis (CMA) is a report that estimates a property's current market value by comparing it to similar properties — called "comps" — that have recently sold, are currently listed, or failed to sell under similar conditions.

The goal of a CMA is to answer one question with data: what is this property actually worth in today's market?

Unlike a formal appraisal — which is conducted by a licensed appraiser and required by lenders for financing — a CMA is prepared by a real estate agent to guide pricing strategy and help sellers understand where their property sits relative to competition and recent sales.

"A CMA is an opinion of value backed by data. An appraisal is a legally defensible conclusion backed by standardized methodology. Both are useful; they serve different purposes."

Agents use CMAs to:

  • Help sellers set an accurate, competitive asking price
  • Win listings by presenting data-backed pricing recommendations
  • Justify a higher price to a skeptical seller who thinks their home is worth more than the market supports
  • Support price reductions when a listing isn't generating showings
  • Educate buyers on why a particular property is priced where it is

The 10-Minute CMA Process

You don't need an hour. You don't need expensive software. Here's the exact 10-minute process used by top-producing agents.

Step 1: Gather Subject Property Data (1 minute)

Before you can compare the property to anything, you need to know exactly what you're working with. Collect:

  • Full address including city, state, and ZIP
  • List price — what the seller is asking (if already listed)
  • Bedrooms and bathrooms — actual count, not what the seller thinks
  • Square footage — from county records or measurement, not the MLS field which is often wrong
  • Year built
  • Lot size
  • Special features — pool, garage, view, smart home, recent renovations, etc.
  • Days on market — if already listed and not under contract

Step 2: Find 3–6 Comparables (3 minutes)

The quality of your CMA is directly tied to the quality of your comps. The rules:

  • Recently sold: Within 90 days. The older the sale, the less relevant it is in a fast-moving market.
  • Within 1 mile: Ideally. For rural areas, expand to comparable neighborhoods with similar characteristics.
  • Same property type: SFR to SFR, condo to condo. Don't compare a single-family home to a townhouse.
  • Similar beds/baths: Within one bedroom count, at minimum.
  • Within 15% of subject price: If your comp is 40% below or above the subject, it's not a true comparison.

Pull at least 3 closed comps (sales that have already recorded) and 2 active listings. The active listings show your competition — where buyers have options today. The closed comps show where the market actually cleared.

Pro Tip: Use Price Per Square Foot as Your Primary Metric

Raw sale price can be misleading because homes vary in size. Price per square foot ($/sqft) levels the playing field and makes it easy to spot outliers. Calculate it for every comp and the subject property — it becomes the foundation of your entire analysis.

Step 3: Make Your Adjustments (3 minutes)

No two homes are identical. Your job is to adjust each comparable's price to reflect how it differs from the subject property. Standard adjustments:

  • Location adjustments: A home with a mountain view is worth more than one facing a busy street. Adjust accordingly.
  • Size adjustments: More square footage = higher value. Typical adjustment: $100–$200 per square foot depending on the market.
  • Bedroom/bath adjustments: Each bedroom adds value. Standard: $10,000–$15,000 per bedroom in most markets. Each bathroom: $5,000–$10,000.
  • Age/condition adjustments: A 1980 home with original kitchen vs. a 2020 home with modern finishes — adjust for the difference in desirability.
  • Feature adjustments: Pool: $15,000–$40,000 depending on climate. Garage: $5,000–$15,000. Smart home: $2,000–$10,000.
  • Market condition adjustment: If the market has shifted since the comp sold (up or down), note this. A comp from 60 days ago in a fast-rising market may be stale.
"A CMA without adjustments is just a list of what other homes sold for. A CMA with thoughtful adjustments is a pricing recommendation grounded in market reality."

Step 4: Calculate the Fair Market Value Range (1 minute)

Once you've adjusted your comps, establish a value range:

  • Low value: Based on the lowest adjusted comp sale — represents the floor of what the market would pay for this property.
  • Mid value: Based on the comps that most closely match the subject — your best estimate of market value.
  • High value: Based on the strongest comps — represents what a premium buyer might pay with the right conditions.

Present these as a range, not a single number. A range gives the seller flexibility and shows you did thorough analysis rather than pulling a number from a crystal ball.

Step 5: Make Your Recommendation (2 minutes)

The CMA is not complete until you've made a recommendation. Based on your analysis, tell the seller:

  • What you recommend their asking price should be
  • Why — cite specific comps, not general market commentary
  • What to expect — days on market, price-to-close ratio, buyer profile

Frame it as: "Based on X recently sold comparables, and Y currently active listings competing with your property, the data supports a list price of $_______. Here's my reasoning..."

Common CMA Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Using Stale Comparables

In a fast-moving market, a comp from 6 months ago can be dramatically out of date. If the market has moved 5–10% since that sale, your CMA is misleading the seller. Always check the days on market for your comps and note the sale date.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Active Competition

Many agents only look at closed sales. But a seller looking at your CMA is asking: "What will buyers see when they shop?" Active listings show what the seller is competing against today — not just where the market was 90 days ago.

Mistake 3: Selecting Comps That Support a Pre-Determined Price

The worst CMAs are the ones where the agent has already decided on a price and then reverse-engineers the comps to justify it. A good CMA lets the data speak. If the comps don't support the price, say so — and explain what price the data actually supports.

Mistake 4: Not Adjusting for Meaningful Differences

Comparing a 4BR/3BA pool home to a 3BR/2BA no-pool home without an adjustment is lazy analysis. Buyers compare homes with different features — your CMA should reflect those differences with dollar adjustments.

Mistake 5: Presenting Without a Recommendation

A CMA is not just a data dump. A seller doesn't know what to do with "here are some homes that sold." They need to know: "Based on this data, here's what you should do." Always close with a clear, specific pricing recommendation.

How to Present a CMA to a Seller

The best CMA in the world is useless if the seller doesn't understand it. Here's how to present it effectively:

  1. Walk through the subject property first. Confirm the details, show you've done your homework on their specific home.
  2. Show the comparables with photos. Visual comparables are more convincing than numbers alone. Buyers respond to what a home looks like.
  3. Walk through your methodology. Explain how you selected the comps, what adjustments you made, and why.
  4. Present the value range. Low, mid, high — with specific comp references for each.
  5. State your recommendation clearly. "Based on this data, I recommend a list price of $________."
  6. Explain what to expect. Days on market, offer price expectations, buyer profile.
  7. Leave the report with them. Give them time to review the data on their own before making a decision.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a CMA in real estate?
A CMA (Comparative Market Analysis) is a report that estimates a property's current market value by comparing it to similar properties that recently sold, are currently listed, or failed to sell under similar conditions. Agents use CMAs to help sellers set an accurate asking price and present data-backed pricing recommendations.

How do you do a CMA in 10 minutes?
Gather subject property data (1 min), find 3–6 comparables from MLS (3 min), make adjustments for differences (3 min), calculate the value range (1 min), and state your recommendation (2 min). The free CMA Report Generator handles the calculation and formatting steps in seconds.

How many comparables do you need for a CMA?
Aim for 3–6 comparable properties: at least 3 recently sold (closed comps within 90 days) and 2–3 currently active listings. The best comps are within 1 mile, same property type, similar bedroom/bath count, and within 15% of the subject's price.

What is the difference between a CMA and an appraisal?
A CMA is prepared by a real estate agent using public data and market expertise — it carries no legal weight for financing decisions. An appraisal is conducted by a state-licensed appraiser following Uniform Residential Appraisal Standards and is required by lenders for purchase transactions. CMAs are for pricing strategy; appraisals are for loan collateral.

What adjustments should you make in a CMA?
Key adjustments include location differences (view, noise, school district), lot size, age and condition, bedroom/bath count, square footage differences, special features (pool, garage, smart home), and market condition corrections. Most agents apply a standard dollar value per bedroom ($10,000–$15,000), per bathroom ($5,000–$10,000), and per square foot ($100–$200) depending on the market.

How do you present a CMA to a seller?
Present the CMA as a professional report with clear sections: subject property summary, comparable sales with photos, pricing analysis, fair market value range, and your specific pricing recommendation. Walk through the numbers verbally, explain your methodology, and always leave the written report with the seller for independent review.
