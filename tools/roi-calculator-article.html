Why Most Investors Don't Calculate ROI Correctly

Here's the mistake I see constantly: an investor sees a property that rents for $2,500/month and costs $2,000/month in mortgage, taxes, insurance, and maintenance. They think — "I'm making $500/month." That's technically true. But it's not ROI.

ROI means Return on Investment — it tells you how efficiently your money is working. If you put $70,000 into that property and make $6,000/year ($500/month × 12), that's an 8.5% cash-on-cash return. Not bad. But if the same $70,000 in a different deal makes $14,000/year, you're leaving money on the table.

Without calculating true ROI, you can't compare deals. You can't know if your portfolio is performing. And you can't make evidence-based decisions about when to hold, sell, or refi.

The Four Numbers That Determine Your Real Estate ROI

A complete ROI calculation includes four components:

  1. Cash Flow — Monthly rent minus all expenses. This is your net operating income before financing.
  2. Equity Build — Principal paydown on your mortgage each month. Every payment you make builds ownership.
  3. Appreciation — The property's value increasing over time. Even at a conservative 2-3% annually, this compounds significantly.
  4. Tax Benefits — Depreciation deductions, mortgage interest write-offs, and 1031 exchange deferrals.

Most investors only look at cash flow. That's why they miss the full picture.

The Simple Formula: Total Return = Cash Flow + Equity + Appreciation

Your actual ROI is the sum of all four components divided by your total cash invested. Anything less than this is an incomplete analysis.

Key Metrics Every Real Estate Investor Must Know

Cash-on-Cash Return

This is the most commonly used metric for rental properties. It's simply your annual cash flow divided by your total cash invested (down payment + renovation costs + closing costs).

Example: You invest $80,000 cash. Your property nets $8,000/year after all expenses. Cash-on-cash return = 10%.

A "good" cash-on-cash return depends on your market and goals, but generally:

  • 0–4% — Below average. Consider negotiating better terms or passing.
  • 5–8% — Acceptable for most markets. Solid but not exceptional.
  • 8–12% — Strong return. This is where good investors target.
  • 12%+ — Excellent. Usually requires creative financing or off-market deals.

Annualized Return (CAGR)

This is the compound annual growth rate — it accounts for appreciation and equity build over time, not just cash flow. Use this when comparing a property you'd hold for multiple years.

A property that appreciates 3%/year and builds equity through mortgage paydown might return 15-18% annually over a 5-year hold — even if the cash-on-cash is only 8%.

Net Profit

Your total profit when you sell: rental income minus expenses minus selling costs (typically 8-10% of sale price for agent fees and closing costs), plus equity and appreciation.

How to Use the ROI Calculator

The calculator above handles all of this automatically. But here's what each input means:

  • Purchase Price — What you paid or plan to pay. Include the full price, not just the loan amount.
  • Renovation Costs — Any repair or improvement costs not covered by the loan. These add to your total cash invested.
  • Down Payment % — The portion of the purchase price you're paying upfront. Higher down payment = lower monthly mortgage but more cash invested.

  • Monthly Expenses — Everything: mortgage payment, property taxes, insurance, HOA, maintenance reserve, vacancy allowance, property management if applicable.
  • Holding Period — How long you plan to hold before selling. This affects annualized return calculations.
  • Expected Sale Price — Conservative estimate of what you'll sell for. Use purchase price × 1.03 if you're unsure (3% annual appreciation is historically conservative).

The Hidden Costs Many Investors Forget

When running your numbers, make sure you include:

  • Vacancy allowance — Budget 5-8% of rent for months the property sits empty between tenants.
  • Maintenance reserve — A general rule: 1% of the property value per year. For a $350,000 home, that's $3,500/year.
  • Property management — If you're not self-managing, budget 8-10% of rent.
  • Capital expenditure (CapEx) — Big repairs: roof, HVAC, water heater. Budget $200-400/month for older properties.
  • Closing costs (buy side) — Typically 2-3% of purchase price in lender fees and title.
  • Closing costs (sell side) — Typically 6-8% in agent fees + 2% in closing costs = 8-10% total.

How to Increase Your ROI After You Buy

Every $100 you add to monthly rent increases your cash-on-cash return by roughly the same as adding $12,000 to your property value (using the 1% rule). So even small rent increases compound over time.

  1. Raise rent smartly — Annual increases of 3-5% keep pace with inflation and market rates. Don't be afraid to push rents — if the market supports it, capture it.
  2. Add income streams — Laundry, parking, pet rent, storage units. Every $50-100/month adds up.
  3. Refinance strategically — If rates drop and you can cut your payment by $200/month without extending your term dramatically, do it.
  4. Renovate for value — Kitchen and bathroom updates typically return 70-80% of cost in increased value.
  5. Shorten your hold period — The faster you can improve and sell, the faster you can deploy capital into the next deal.

Quick Reference: What "Good" ROI Looks Like in 2026

Market conditions change. Here's what to expect in current market conditions:

  • Cash-on-cash return: 6-10% is a solid target for most US markets. Higher in lower-cost markets, lower in coastal metros.
  • Total ROI (5-year hold): 50-100% is achievable on well-chosen properties with moderate appreciation.
  • Annualized return: 10-15% is a reasonable expectation for a balanced (cash flow + appreciation) scenario.

These are general guidelines — your specific numbers depend on your local market, financing terms, and the deals you find.
